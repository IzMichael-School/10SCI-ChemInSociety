Silicon & Coal

Silicon - #1

Group IVA elements include carbon germanium, tin, and lead. Silicon is the fourteenth element on the periodic table. Dark grey in colour, silicon has the same crystalline structure as diamond. This substance has comparable chemical and physical characteristics. 1410° C (2570° F) is the melting point of silicon, whereas 2355° C (4271° F) is the boiling point, and it has a density of 2.33 grammes per cubic centimetre.

Silicon - #2

A pure form of silicon is extremely rare, yet it is the second most abundant element in the Earth's crust. Silicon, ferrosilicon, and silicon metal are all produced in China, which is by far the world's greatest producer of it. On the worldwide scale, China generated almost two-thirds of the seven million tonnes of silicon manufactured that year, with 4.5 million metric tonnes of silicon produced in 2019. Russia, Norway, and the United States are other important producers.

Silicon - #3

Approximately 320,000 metric tonnes of silicon were produced in the United States in 2019. Silicon and silicon metal exports were over 10,000 metric tonnes each. U.S. imports ferrosilicon and silicon metal averaging around 140,000 metric tonnes. Approximately 20 percent of U.S. silicon imports came from Russia between 2015 and 2018.

Silicon - #4

A total of 560,000 metric tonnes of silicon were consumed in the United States in 2019. Alloys and other undefined categories utilise silicon in the manufacture of steel and cast iron. Intriguingly, silicon is also the building block for semiconductor polymers, which are ubiquitous. The solar industry relies heavily on polysilicon, a particularly pure type of silicon. In electronics, silicon is commonly used as semiconductors for discrete devices and integrated circuits.

Coal - #1

Coal has had a huge effect on the world's energy supply since the commencement of industrialisation in the 19th century. Coal is still one of the most important non-renewable energy sources today. In recent years, the usage of hard coal, the most significant form of coal, has even risen a bit. Among all non-renewable energy sources, coal has by far the greatest reserve base.

Coal - #2

A steady increase in coal output has been observed over the past few years, and in 2019 it reached almost 168 exajoules. Countries that produce the most hard coal include China (first), India (second), the US, Australia (third) and Indonesia (fourth). India's state-owned Coal India Limited produced 606.9 million metric tonnes of coal in 2018/19, making it the world's largest producer.

Coal - #3

Founded in 1975, the firm is among the world's top 10 pollutants. Arch Coal, located in the United States, is another worldwide leader in coal mining, and it was one of the world's most valuable public businesses in March 2020. U.S. coal mining is centred on Appalachian states like West Virginia, Kentucky and Pennsylvania. Wyoming is home to the bulk of the country's largest coal mines.

Coal - #4

Despite the fact that Central and South America's coal demand grew the most from 2018 to 2019, ambitious economic heavyweights China and India continue to use the bulk of the coal. Coal accounts for 60 percent of China's fast increasing energy demand. Despite a modest decline in coal usage in the United States during the previous decade, other estimates foresee a large increase in coal consumption through 2030.

Compare

Contrasting the two resources, Silicon and Coal, we can see that while silicon is in high demand, it is also being collected sustainably, and efficiently. Coal, on the other hand, is being farmed and used in a way that is harming the world, and in a way that will cause our supplies to expire.
